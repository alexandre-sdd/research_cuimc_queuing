\section{Purpose and Modeling Choice}

This note defines the first appointment simulation used in the project after simplifying the
time scale to one decision epoch per day. The simulator still keeps enough patient-level audit
information to compute delay, cancellation, no-show, and utilization metrics, but the model itself
is now written in day-level terms.

\begin{keybox}[title={Core Choice}]
The simulation clock is the day \(D\). The state remembers how many class-1 and class-2 patients
are scheduled for each future day. Slot positions inside a day are implementation detail used only
to enforce daily capacity and report utilization.
\end{keybox}

This is the formulation to keep in mind:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \(D\) is the current day.
    \item \(S\) is the number of appointment slots available per day.
    \item \(H\) is the rolling booking horizon in days.
    \item \(i\in\{1,2\}\) is the patient class, with room to add more classes later.
    \item \(r\in\{0,1,\dots,H-1\}\) is residual delay, so \(r=0\) means appointments scheduled for today.
    \item \(\tau\) is the original delay offered when the patient booked.
\end{itemize}

The distinction between \(r\) and \(\tau\) remains important. Residual delay \(r\) belongs to the
daily state. Original offered delay \(\tau\) is stored with a booked patient because balking and
no-show behavior depend on the delay the patient accepted.

\section{Model Objects}

\subsection{Day-Level State}

At the start of day \(D\), after the day's cancellation step and before new arrivals are processed,
define
\[
X_{i,r}^{D}
=
\text{number of class-}i\text{ patients scheduled for day }D+r.
\]

The state vector is
\[
X^D = \left(X_{i,r}^{D}: i\in\mathcal I,\ r=0,\dots,H-1\right),
\]
where \(\mathcal I=\{1,2\}\) in the current implementation. This notation naturally extends to
more classes by enlarging \(\mathcal I\).

Daily capacity imposes
\[
\sum_{i\in\mathcal I} X_{i,r}^{D} \le S
\qquad
\text{for every }r.
\]

The implementation may still keep an internal slot list for each day in order to identify open
capacity and produce slot-level logs. That internal list is not part of the mathematical state.
Only the class counts by day matter for the model.

\subsection{Patient Record}

When a patient accepts an offer, the simulator records the patient as
\[
(i,\tau),
\]
where \(i\) is the class and \(\tau\) is the original offered delay. This record is carried until
the patient cancels, no-shows, is served, or remains scheduled at the simulation end.

The patient record is not meant to reintroduce a slot index into the model. It exists because:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \(b_i(\tau)\) determines balking at booking time;
    \item \(\xi_i(\tau)\) determines no-show at appointment time;
    \item optional cancellation rules may depend on both \(\tau\) and current residual delay \(r\);
    \item logs need patient-level timing for auditing and metrics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Classes and Behavior}

Each patient class is described by
\[
C_i = \{\lambda_i, b_i(\tau), q_i \text{ or } \phi_i(\tau,r), \xi_i(\tau)\}.
\]

The terms are:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{L{0.22\textwidth} L{0.68\textwidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Object} & \textbf{Meaning} \\
\midrule
\(\lambda_i\) & expected number of class-\(i\) arrivals per day \\
\addlinespace
\(b_i(\tau)\) & probability that class-\(i\) declines an offered appointment with delay \(\tau\) \\
\addlinespace
\(q_i\) & constant daily cancellation probability for class-\(i\) scheduled patients \\
\addlinespace
\(\phi_i(\tau,r)\) & optional custom daily cancellation rule using original delay and current residual delay \\
\addlinespace
\(\xi_i(\tau)\) & no-show probability at appointment time, conditional on the patient still being scheduled \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

The baseline cancellation input is the scalar \(q_i\). It is applied directly once per day to each
scheduled patient considered in the cancellation step. If a richer cancellation function is supplied,
the simulator evaluates that function as \(\phi_i(\tau,r)\).

For the current two-class experiments, demand can also be parameterized by a total daily arrival
rate \(\lambda\) and a class-1 share \(p\):
\[
\lambda_1 = p\lambda,
\qquad
\lambda_2 = (1-p)\lambda.
\]

\section{Daily Simulation Order}

The simulator advances one day at a time. For each day \(D\), it executes the following steps in
this order.

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.35em]
    \item \textbf{Compute cancellations for the day.} Each already scheduled patient independently cancels with daily probability \(q_i\), or with \(\phi_i(\tau,r)\) if a custom rule is supplied. Canceled appointments are removed from the schedule before any new arrivals are handled.
    \item \textbf{Simulate arrivals.} For every class,
    \[
    M_i^D \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_i),
    \]
    where \(M_i^D\) is the number of class-\(i\) arrivals on day \(D\).
    \item \textbf{Offer future days in FCFS order.} Individual arrivals are processed sequentially in random within-day order. The baseline policy offers the earliest future day \(D+r\), with \(r\in\{1,\dots,H-1\}\), that still has open capacity.
    \item \textbf{Simulate balking.} If a class-\(i\) patient is offered delay \(\tau=r\), the patient accepts with probability \(1-b_i(\tau)\). If the patient balks, the offered capacity remains available to later arrivals.
    \item \textbf{Simulate no-shows for day \(D\).} After booking decisions are complete, each patient still scheduled for day \(D\) no-shows with probability \(\xi_i(\tau)\) and is served otherwise.
    \item \textbf{Compute metrics for day \(D\).} The simulator records arrivals, booked patients, balking, no-offer events, cancellations, no-shows, served patients, empty slots, utilization, and delay metrics for the measured window.
    \item \textbf{Advance to day \(D+1\).} The current day is removed from the rolling horizon, residual delays decrease by one, and a new empty future day is appended.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{keybox}[title={Event Order}]
\begin{align*}
&\text{cancellations}
\rightarrow
\text{arrivals}
\rightarrow
\text{future-day FCFS offers}
\rightarrow
\text{balking}\\
&\rightarrow
\text{no-shows/service}
\rightarrow
\text{metrics}
\rightarrow
\text{next day}.
\end{align*}
\end{keybox}

Same-day offers are not allowed in this baseline. If a cancellation opens capacity for day \(D\),
that capacity can reduce empty slots for day \(D\), but new arrivals on day \(D\) are still offered
only days \(D+1,\dots,D+H-1\).

\section{FCFS Booking Rule}

Let
\[
N_r^D = \sum_{i\in\mathcal I} X_{i,r}^{D}
\]
be the number of scheduled patients on day \(D+r\) after the cancellation step.

For an arriving patient on day \(D\), the baseline FCFS rule searches
\[
r=1,2,\dots,H-1
\]
and offers the first day with
\[
N_r^D < S.
\]

If no such day exists, the patient receives no offer. If a day is offered and accepted, the
appointment is added to the count for that future day and the patient's original offered delay is
stored as \(\tau=r\).

\begin{examplebox}[title={One-Day Example}]
Suppose \(S=3\), \(H=4\), and after cancellations on day \(D\) the aggregate scheduled counts are
\[
N_0^D=2,\qquad N_1^D=3,\qquad N_2^D=1,\qquad N_3^D=0.
\]
Today has one open slot, but same-day offers are disabled. Tomorrow is full. The first bookable day
is therefore \(D+2\), so a new arrival is offered \(\tau=2\). If the patient accepts, the simulator
increments the class-specific count for residual day \(r=2\) and stores the patient record
\((i,2)\).
\end{examplebox}

\section{Metrics}

For patients arriving during the measured window, the simulator reports:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{L{0.28\textwidth} L{0.62\textwidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Definition} \\
\midrule
Arrivals & number of appointment requests \\
\addlinespace
Booked & number of requests that accept an offer \\
\addlinespace
Balked & number of requests that receive an offer and decline it \\
\addlinespace
No offer & number of requests for which no future capacity is available \\
\addlinespace
Canceled & number of booked patients who cancel before service resolution \\
\addlinespace
No-shows & number of booked patients who remain scheduled but do not attend \\
\addlinespace
Served & number of booked patients who attend \\
\addlinespace
Mean booked delay & average original offered delay \(\tau\) among booked patients \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

The main ratios are
\[
\frac{\mathrm{Booked}}{\mathrm{Arrivals}},
\qquad
\frac{\mathrm{Served}}{\mathrm{Booked}},
\qquad
\frac{\mathrm{Booked\ within\ target}}{\mathrm{Arrivals}}.
\]

For measured service days, slot-based utilization still uses \(S\) as daily capacity:
\[
\rho^{\mathrm{booked}}
=
\frac{\mathrm{booked\ slots}}{S\times \mathrm{measured\ days}},
\qquad
\rho^{\mathrm{attended}}
=
\frac{\mathrm{served\ slots}}{S\times \mathrm{measured\ days}}.
\]

\section{Implementation Mapping}

The code follows this day-level formulation:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{arrival\_rate} is \(\lambda_i\), the expected class-\(i\) arrivals per day.
    \item \texttt{cancel\_probability} as a scalar is the direct daily cancellation probability \(q_i\).
    \item \texttt{tau\_offered} and \texttt{tau\_booked} are original offered delays in days.
    \item \texttt{arrival\_slot} is retained as a legacy log column and is empty under the day-level process.
    \item \texttt{state\_log} records \(X_{i,r}^{D}\) for every configured class and residual day.
    \item \texttt{slots\_per\_day} is daily capacity \(S\), not a time index used for arrivals.
\end{itemize}

The implementation keeps an internal list of \(S\) slots per day only to identify open capacity,
preserve appointment-level audit records, and compute empty-slot and utilization metrics.

\section{Relation to the Literature}

The appointment-queueing papers that motivate this project usually compress the schedule into a
queue length, backlog, or appointment-window state. The day-level simulator is a discrete
calendar version of the same mechanism:
\begin{align*}
\text{more backlog}
&\Rightarrow
\text{larger offered delay}\\
&\Rightarrow
\text{more balking, cancellation, or no-show risk}\\
&\Rightarrow
\text{lower realized service}.
\end{align*}

Green and Savin (2008), Liu and Ziya (2014), and Liu (2015) use stylized queueing states to study
delay-sensitive attendance and capacity decisions. Here, the state \(X_{i,r}^{D}\) keeps the
calendar interpretation explicit while avoiding unnecessary slot notation in the model.
